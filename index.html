Cost borne by retirees
Implementing policies such as increasing the state retirement age or removing the triple lock guarantee would improve pension sustainability, at the cost of current retirees:
  • The removal of the triple lock or increasing the retirement age would decrease the costs of the pension system, making it more resilient in the short term against the UK's demographic changes.
  • However these policies come at a clear trade-off to current retirees, as they would reduce the value of pensions or delay access to income, potentially increasing financial insecurity and lowering living standards among pensioners.

Cost borne by taxpayers
Increasing National Insurance Contributions would directly raise revenue for the pension system, improving its sustainability without harming retirees:
  • However, raising taxes on workers at a time when the UK is recovering from a cost of living crisis would put additional pressure on households already facing poor wage growth.
  • In addition, the workers funding the current system through higher contributions are unlikely to receive the same levels of benefits they are paying into, given the demographic pressures that will inevitably reshape the pension system.

Cost borne by future generations
Rather than reforming the pension system today, the UK could continue funding pensions through government borrowing, deferring the cost to future taxpayers who have no say in decisions being made now:
  • Maintaining the current system without reform allows today's pensioners and workers to avoid immediate pain, by adding to the national debt which future generations will be required to service and repay.
  • This is not a neutral choice, compound interest means deferred costs grow over time, and the UK's national debt already exceeds 100% of GDP. This limits the government's ability to spend on other priorities.
